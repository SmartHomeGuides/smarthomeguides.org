## A day in an automated home

Let us walk through a regular day. Not in some futuristic showcase, but in a normal home where a few things just happen on their own.

<div class="not-prose border-l-2 border-primary/30 ml-4 my-6">
  <TimelineEvent time="7:00 AM" icon="sun">
    Your alarm goes off. The bedroom blinds slowly open, letting in natural
    light. The hallway light turns on at a soft 20%. The coffee machine starts
    brewing. You did not schedule any of this manually. Your home knows it is a
    weekday and someone just woke up.
  </TimelineEvent>
  <TimelineEvent time="8:15 AM" icon="door-open">
    You leave for work. The front door locks behind you. The thermostat drops a
    few degrees to save energy. The lights turn off, one by one. The robot
    vacuum starts its daily round.
  </TimelineEvent>
  <TimelineEvent time="5:45 PM" icon="house">
    You are ten minutes from home. The thermostat starts warming things up. The
    porch light turns on because the sun has already set.
  </TimelineEvent>
  <TimelineEvent time="10:30 PM" icon="moon">
    Bedtime. You say goodnight, or just go to bed. The house takes care of the
    rest: doors lock, lights off, alarm system armed, thermostat drops to
    sleeping temperature.
  </TimelineEvent>
</div>

None of this required an app or a voice command. The home just followed simple rules based on time, presence, and habits.

## What can you automate?

Here is a practical overview, organized by what it does for your daily life:

| Category           | What it does                                  | Real examples                                                                                                     | Impact                                |
| ------------------ | --------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------- |
| **Lighting**       | Lights respond to time, presence, or activity | Sunset triggers porch lights. Motion sensor lights the hallway at night. Lights off when everyone leaves.         | No more switches to flip or forget    |
| **Climate**        | Heating and cooling adapt automatically       | Thermostat lowers when you leave, warms up before you return. Different temperatures per room.                    | Comfortable without thinking about it |
| **Security**       | Locks and cameras act on their own            | Door auto-locks at night. Camera sends an alert for unexpected motion. Doorbell notifies you with a snapshot.     | Peace of mind, not paranoia           |
| **Daily routines** | Multiple actions from a single trigger        | "Good morning" opens blinds, starts coffee, plays the news. "Good night" locks up and dims everything.            | One moment replaces five manual steps |
| **Energy**         | Track and reduce waste automatically          | Smart plugs turn off idle appliances. Energy monitor shows what is costing you. Lights off in empty rooms.        | Lower bills with zero effort          |
| **Presence**       | The home reacts to who is here                | Adjusts lighting, music, and temperature based on who is home. Sends an alert if nobody is home and a door opens. | No more "did I leave the lights on?"  |

<Callout type="tip" title="Start with what annoys you">
  You do not need all of this. Look at the table above and find the one thing
  that frustrates you most: the light you always forget, the thermostat you keep
  adjusting, the door you are never sure you locked. Start there.
</Callout>

## It is not about the gadgets

The point is not to fill your home with technology. It is to solve real, everyday problems quietly. A single motion sensor in a dark hallway might be the most useful automation you ever set up, and it costs less than a dinner out.

In the next chapter, we will talk about _how_ to think about automation so you get the most value without overcomplicating things.
